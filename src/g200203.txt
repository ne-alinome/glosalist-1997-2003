   I would rather see it as research into new subject matter: a tutorial
written by Sid, me, or anyone else.
   But I am not in favour of rehashing the "18 Steps to Fluency in Glosa."
I would rather leave this historical work in the archive area, and not play
around with it.
   This suggestion, of going through a current attempt to use Glosa, does
appeal to me.  It reminds me of the way I used to do amateur teaching of
English to Asians: I would ask someone to volunteer a sentence; then I
would write it on the white-board exactly how they said it; next, I would
ask the other learners for suggestions on ways of improving it.

to me that the whole lexicon ought to be standardised prior to commencing
the 'debugging' of one, or more, tutorials.

as it stands.  However, with the inputs from Wendy and Marcel in
straightening out the last few ambiguities, I would suggest that this
revision be completed before tutors are written.

suggests that a tutor written using the present Glo vocab would be a bit
hard on the reader.  I suspect that Glo needs a bit of fleshing out: the
ribs are showing through; it feels just a bit lean for comfort.  Maybe it
ought to be 600 words rather than 500.

As I understand it ~ge~ is not a word in Glosa, but
only a prefix (ie ~ge-~ {with hyphen}).

~gene~ is a word in Glosa meaning -
            "get, receive, acquire, become or obtain" 
               a "thing, quality or state"

~Mi pa gene u grama.~
I received a letter.
I was sent a letter.

~Id gene frigi.~
It's getting cold.
It's becoming cold.
It's receiving coldness.

~U glacia pa gene liqi.~
The ice melted.
The ice became liquid.
The ice acquired a state of liquidity.

~Fe gene sko de Glosa~
She is learning Glosa.
She is getting instruction about Glosa.
She is being taught Glosa.

~U fenestra pa gene frakti~
The window was broken.
The window got broken.
The window became broken.
The window received a breaking.
  {ie was the 'victim' of the breaking.}

He was born 5th December.
He got born 5th December.
He became born 5th December.
He received birth 5th December.

Mi Plu Amika,
  Here is an edited version of my earlier attempt to casting the first
third of the _Tri Ursus_ story into Glo.

  It seems to be working.

  The Bears' house is at the end of the path, which does not simply pass by
their home.

  I have gone along with most of Kevin's suggested alterations, but
probably wish to retain responsibility for both Glosa and Glo renditions.

  Needless to say the original, possibly Evanescent - if not Old -
 Glosa version of _Tri Ursus_ will come up for review, soon.  I would guess
that this ought to be an exercise for all on Glosalist.  It could possibly
be edited in parts, as is being done with the Glo version.  Any suggestions?

  In the Glo version, we changed the season from Spring to Autumn.

  I would like to see 'equal and opposite action' in Glo, so suggest a
"push" to balance Glo's "pull" of ~tira~ or ~trakto~.  It could be GID's
either ~impakti~ or ~pulsa~, but I favour the archaic Old Glosa ``peli``.
The Latin dictionary mentions  impello  and  expello  as well as  impulsus
among its renditions of "push."

  Closing things .. as well as having them open [Glo:aperi] .. seems also
in need of being covered - possibly with ~klude~.

  A bit of soft usage comes with the easy-to-say ~in qi~, whereas, as Kevin
Points out, ~intra qi~ is the '''correct''' form.  I first noticed such
?slackness? when it came from the Glosa authors; however, I decided that
this must be another of their intuitions about the rounding-off effect of
usage over time.  So, while ~intra qi~ [inside which], is the correct form,
the average Glosa speaker is more than likely to 'lose' the rest of the
word, and soften it to the more-easily-said, but incorrect, ~in qi~ [into
which].

  I look forward to seeing the adjusted version of the Glo vocab.

Still TriBrunoZoa 0.3-->1 to go,

Robin Gaskell

sending "plaintext" and "Attachments" as well as I can. R.

According to GID:

~de~ is: about; belong; concerning; from; in relation to;

~ex~ is: out from; by (marker of doer by passive voice);

In particular, note that "from" appears in both lists. I'm
trying to figure out how to describe how someone would
choose one over the other.

It seems that ~ex~ is used for authorship and emergence
(the crew came out of the ship).  

It also has "out", like ~u ex-urba area~ (the area outside
the city). Normally I would expect that to be ~extra~, but
I suppose this is an abbreviation. Perhaps in Glo I could
combine ~ex~ and ~extra~, like I am considering with ~in~
and ~intra~.

ex-employee (someone who used to be an employee), which I
guess is related to the "since" that is listed. But those
two don't seem to fit the rest of the definition. In ~mi pa
ze vora ex pa-di~, why would this use ~ex~?

I guess it's like I haven't eaten "outside" yesterday.
Seems like a metaphorical stretch. And we have "po" which
seems better anyway.

Now, ~de~ is for belonging (like a dog _of_ mine), and
concerning (like a book _concerning_ food). The "than"
merely seems to reflect that ~de~ fills in where no other
little word fits, like in ~ma sito de~. 

I suppose if I say I "_am_ from Seattle", that would imply
a belonging, rather than a trip origination point, and so
would be ~de~. So I guess we have:

~mi pa veni _ex_ u Texas~

~mi es _de_ u Texas~

Glosa sentence, even when the writer cuts some corners.

I should point out that it was not Charles who originally
mentioned the confusion. And the fault certainly did NOT
lie with the reader. The problem was that the sentence
itself was confusing.

In this case, I could not put ~u/un~ before ~auxi~, because
I wanted it to be a verb, not a noun. Mentally, I used the
omission of a PoS marker to indicate its verbness.

You suggest adding PoS markers "If there might be any
confusion...". But the problem in this case is that I (the
writer) incorrectly believed that there could not be any
confusion. I really thought I had written an unambiguous
sentence. Now, If I had gone back and thought about it for
five more minutes, perhaps I would have caught my two
errors.

That's the danger of omitting "u" and "nu": As the author
(or speaker), you know what you intend to say, so your mind
can fill in the missing pieces. The reader (or listener)
may have a completely different perspective. Thus, I think
PoS markers (u/plu, pa/nu/fu) should be used in almost all
cases.

There are a few certain words that imply a PoS, and
therefore don't need an explicit one. Pronouns, I think,
can work without u. And I think we have a consensus that
~es~ does not need a pa/nu/fu because it can "only" be a
verb. There may be a few other exceptions, but I can't
think of them right now.

Several other particles share that function, of course.

That's the true meaning of U/UN and PLU. As with the verbal
particles, Glosa's noun-phrase particles can be arranged in
a fairly simple paradigm, the relevant items here being:

1. case DE AD etc.
2. number U/UN PLU
3. deictic/definite CI LA

One may question whether items 2 and 3 are ordered properly,

By "article" is meant a particle to indicate definiteness versus
indefiniteness. Some languages have a definite article, the lack of
which indicates indefiniteness; the reverse strategy is also attested.
If "U/UN" were Glosa's true article (which it is not) it would translate
as English "the" or "a/an", but certainly not both. It is actually just
the mark of singular number for its associated noun, though as a happy
side-effect one also can infer the presence of a noun phrase.

> The U-LA or U-CI or PLU-CI or PLU-LA quartet are the impersonal pronouns
> that indicate if the THING,or THINGS, in question, are HERE or THERE.

It is well-known that the definite article generally evolves from
"deictics" or "demonstratives" (or whatever term you prefer). Clearly
in Glosa's case the purely "deictic" particles CI and LA are adjoined
to the purely singular/plural particles to compose the demonstratives.
Since I can translate "the" and "a/an" using them, and since the Glosa
documentation *does* refer to them as "articles", I'll continue to
call U-LA and brethren the nearest thing to true articles in G.

I disagree. In order to be able to quickly read Glosa text,

Some languages use suffixes, so by looking at a single word

Robin Gaskell wrote
>   Can we answer the question, 'is syntax ^hard-wired^ into the brain?';

> regularised syntax for Glosa???

One can say that syntax is hard-wired in the brain, in the sense that
every language has a syntax. But not every language has the same
syntax. What is hard-wired is that there are certain possible patterns,
and certain impossible patterns. For language X the pattern is A,
and for language Y the pattern is the not A. And the pattern we learn
as children, seems to us to be the "natural" pattern.

Interglossa had a very rigidly defined syntax, which was clear but made 
it seem cold and clinical and rather boring. Glosa went to the other 
extreme and was very flexible. All sorts of possibilities were tried out,
and some were kept, some were thrown out, and some revisions
were made over the years of development.

What one needs to do, is to look at real sentences as they are now, 
and to draw out the rules that underlie the constructions, rather than 
invent a logical framework and try to force Glosa into it.

   - I am going back through old downloads to see what I missed -

   Many items on your list are still hazy, and not set in concrete, but
they could be worked on .. though not endlessly.

   When I last sought authoritative work on the subtle level of universal
syntax rules I did not get far - I might try again.  Then, this could be a
first for Glosa: maybe these syntax universals have not been tackled by the
Linguists, yet.

   Punctuation is reasonably thought out in Glosa, with some dislike of the
semi-colon - on beauty points, I fear.  But, in Glosa, which does rely on
word order and structure within sentences, I believe that punctuation is
much more important than it is, say, in English.  For this reason, I
believe that the writer should use suitable punctuation to indicate the
start of phrases and clauses - where an absence of punctuation could cause
ambiguity.  In English, the current fashion is against using commas
'sculpturally', but I think we cannot afford such whimsy in a language with
syntax-based grammar.

   More work needed, with example needed to demonstrate compounding.  Use
or non-use of the hyphen should be clear-cut.  Possibly a special sort of
grouping mark could be used to show that a set of unhyphenated substantives
comprised a compound.
  EG  He turned on the _wind monitoring hurricane detector_ just in time to
predict that the next tornado was only two hours away.

   THe rule of thumb on phrase markers is that a comma indicates the start
of a new phrase - but this is only essential in complicated sentences, and
the comma can be elided in simple sentences.  The other idea is that the
next stage up, the semi-colon, acts as a guidepost to the start of a
clause.  This is a reasonable rule, but, where an excess of semi-colons
might seem a little heavy-handed, commas will often handle the start of a
new clause.  If a standard rule can be derived, then it would be a good
idea, but we must always leve people the option of eliding non-essential
punctuation.

   It goes without saying that I cannot start pulling your leg in polite
society, if some present do not know that I am using an English idiom.
However, it is a bit high-handed of us to rule idioms out completely,
especially if they are the point of a joke, or necessary to give colour to
a story.  As long as there is a way of indicating the presence of
non-literal language, then people will attempt to read an idiom in context,
or will know to reach for their dictionary of idiomatic phrases.
  EG.  Joe pa dice ke an pa du es ^trakto mu plu poda^ tem u pa horo, e u
holo history pa es ge-face ex an.

   This clear definition of words has taken up far too much time, already,
and should be finalised before the middle of this year.  Wow, did I say
that!?  Looks like those of us active in this are will have to really
complete the job.
