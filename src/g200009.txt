> ~Kani, qi morda, es u menace.~       Biting dogs are a menace.   (ie dogs that bite)

According to the Authorities (originally Hogben),
it should be "kani; qi ..." but such abuse of a
semicolon is too foolish to even discuss.

> ~Morda kani es u menace.~    Biting dogs is a menace. (ie people that bite dogs)

Infinitive: MORDARE kani es ...

> ~Morda ex kani es u menace.~   Biting dogs are a menace.   (ie dogs that bite)

Active participle: MORDANTE kani ...

Of course, this would spoil Glosa's ability to claim that it has no grammar
or at least no morphology. But I think the authors A&C never really intended
it as a true pidgin-creole, but rather as a semi-naive relexing of English.
Hogben meant something different altogether, but his ideas were just
the outline of a very strange project, one that appealed strongly to me
30 years ago, and seems quite silly now; A&C improved it greatly.

> ~Morda de kani es u menace.~   Biting dogs is a menace.   (ie people that bite dogs)

I think the more international usage is "Morda A/AD ..."

> > > > > ~Galina ne es boni kaso kani vora~    Chicken isn't good as a dog meal.
> > > > > (~vora~ = eat = eating = a meal)

> > Any run of morphologically undistinguished roots is ambiguous;

> True. I was attempting to use ~kaso~ prepositionally here, meaning "in the
> case of". Would perhaps ~Galina ne es boni de kani vora~  have been clearer?

I would try "Galina ne es boni PRO ...".

More likely the authors took MANGIARE from Italian.
MANG- looks like "mango". Maybe the Glosa phonology
simply does not support (should not allow) MANGIA.
Or, my proposal to use J could be invoked: MANJA.
Then there's a conflict with MANIA unless J is
always pronounced as soft-G, as it *should* be.

Glosa (Ron Clark) chose to eliminate doubled letters
and use K for hard-C. Such in-my-opinion misguided
phoneticization generates many problems like these.
I suppose the answer is to eliminate all troublesome
roots like MANGIA, because others can always be found
that *will* fit his procrustean orthography.

I am re-reading some of the early Kosmoglott magazines,
edited by Edgar De Wahl, deviser of Occidental/Interlingue.
In those days the IAL options were mainly Espo-Ido,
Peano's Interlingua, and Occidental in-between. In my
previous reading I skipped anything not concerning OCC,
but there were many articles by Peano and others about
(and/or written in) their own language projects.
This time I'm reading them all ... except Esperanto,
which I cannot penetrate due to its mangled roots.

Besides Interlingua's great resemblance to Glosa,
as remarked by somebody on this list earlier, I find
some other interesting foreshadowings of Interglossa/Glosa.
These were well-received and never criticized by OCC-ists;
I think EDW would be more "analytical" today than he felt
was possible then, due to prevalence of Latin in schools.

Most of this is rather easy to understand at first sight,
but some details are difficult. The most radical of the
writers is Nik Yucmanof of Petropol, in his all-lower-case
(proletarian?) language/notation "Etem". Sometimes he revues
other langs of similar type, one of which is called "Glott"
and has some interesting parallels with Glosa. (So here I'm
trying to revue a revue of a lang in a lang in a lang!)

: "glott" usu alfabet anglo-latin

: gramatik de "glott" es analitic.
: numer es indikat per numerativ (du soldat)
: aut per artikul: definit "la" (sing.), "les" (pl.),
: indefinit "un" (sing.), "plures" (pl.);
: deklin es fakat per "preposision":
: "de la patre", "ad la matre"", ...;

(More stuff too similar to Glosa deleted)

: adverbi et adjektiv ne es diferensiat;
: verb es interesant: radik de id es ekstrakt
: eks parol intereuropean ko "-or, -iv, -ur, ...",
: ergo: "amat, skript, decis, evolut ...";

(In other words, verbs are taken from the Latin
perfective/supine as is usual in English. Good idea!)

: sintakt es preferet logikal, simil a tip angl.
: stil es mult klar.

"Still"? Hmph.

: parol-formasion es simpl: sufiks et prefiks
: korespond a id de "occidental"

Which in turn corresponds to Espo-Ido.
In those days nobody dared present a project
lacking a credible system for word-derivation.

: selekt de leksik es tal: radik et parol,
: difus in omni lingu european, es akseptat
...
: in mank de parol inter-european, on selekt radik,
: komun a du famili lingual: "automne" (roman et angl),
: "fenestre" (germano-roman), "columbe" (romano-slav)

That's a refreshingly simple and effective algorithm.

This all confirms the "all eurolangs are dialects of
a single international lang" hypothesis. Nothing is
really new about Glosa, and previous analytic projects
have been better in some important ways.

Hogben wrote in his book "INTERGLOSSA" (chapter 2) -

"Since Interglossa is an isolating (analytical) language, learning
Interglossa involves learning merely: (a) its etymology, i.e. mnemonic
association of each vocable to an internationally current root (Chapter XI);
(b) its semantics, i.e. analysis of the meaningful content of the vocables;
(c) its word-order; (d) its phonetics and typography."

This indicates to me [see (a) above] that he expected people to be familiar
with words like "telephone" and "microscope", whilst not knowing the
meanings of the elements that make up these words, since he says they must
"learn" the etymology.

"With a few exceptions the vocables of Interglossa are based on unmutilated
roots of words which now belong to the vocabulary of all countries where
modern technology and hygiene have penetrated. The meaning ascribed to any
one of them does not necessarily tally with the one given in a Latin or a
Greek lexicon. It is the meaning suggested by the internationally current
words in which it occurs."

This again indicates that the basis of Interglossa was this "international"
vocabulary and not a knowledge of Latin and Greek words.

Ashby & Clark, writing in "CENTRAL GLOSA" (page 9), give as the second
educational benefit of learning Glosa that -

"The great number of technical terms a modern student has to comprehend and
memorize get meaning through Glosa and so become easily memorable."

This must mean that they, like Hogben, did not believe that people already
knew the meaning of the Greek and Latin roots that make up these technical
terms.

How do you make that happen?  Moderated lists are troublesome
things.  Lotta work, lotta responsibility.  I don't think anyone
has succeeded as you intend to do.  Good luck.

One approach, possibly, would be to require the discussions to be
in "Occidental, Glosa, etc".  That would provide a barrier to idle
entry and somewhat discourage facile controversy.  Those who had
the impertinence to design a language for others without knowing a
couple of IALs themselves would choose to go elsewhere.  Those who
knew a couple would be able to handle any of the "etc" languages
that met your criteria, not without a little effort, but with a
degree of it acceptable and interesting to language designers.

That approach has, of course, been tried in the bablo list, with
only moderate success, but the bablani are by definition a less
expert group, and there is not the restriction to naturalistic
languages that you contemplate.

If you want to do it in English in an international mailing list
you need to justify the use of English as an IAL.  There is some
incongruity in an English-language list of this kind, I think.

The "naturalistic" is the bugbear.  It is there I think you will
go wrong.  I don't know if you have a clear meaning for it.  Does
it mean there will be excessively complex, or undefined, rules of
derivation, or will the words (as I think you intend) be
invariable?  Does it mean just that the vocabulary will come from
a few languages that arose in one part of the globe?  In other
words, the contrary of a priori?  If so, perhaps there is a better
word than "naturalistic".

Basically, I'm convinced that a naturalistic language can't be a
world language.  What is needed is an autonomous language, one
amenable to complete definition, and indeed, completely defined.  
Not a semi-artificial languages.

Personally I'm pretty clueless, but the only world languages I
know of are Esperanto, Ido, and Glosa.  And I'm doubtful about the
last.  Occidental is not a candidate, mea opinione.

rather than via a formula.

>facia           face, features, surface

*   I'd say that this U was one too many.

 Intra u su facia de u gra agri, na detekti plu kilo de pusi insekti.

>euro-pidgin (bene-face, lega-face, fabri-domi, epi-face)
>or anglo-latin (beneficial, surface, facetious, invention).
>I'd really like to know how others see it ...
>
*   For the first question, I would take 50-50 odds: for vocabulary, Glosa
is a sort of Classico-creole; while for its construction, it is more Anglo,
but not very Latin.
    Imagine a time .. in prehistory .. when early man though little before
uttering, and barely distinguished between things and their actions.  In
such a mystic stage of evolution, the concepts echoing thought were also
closely related to the sounds used to convey these thoughts: it is
postulated, here, that proto-man used proto-language in a very loose way,
such that metaphorical usage blurred the boundaries between the concrete
and the abstract, and simple, largely un-inflected sounds were used to
string these thoughts together.
    Now imagine a time .. in post-history .. when civilised man painfully,
urgently seeks to rediscover the fundamentals of communication to
facilitate intercommunication between groups whose long linguistic
evolutions have driven them apart.  In such a time there will be attempts
to impose the most evolved of civilisation's national languages on all;
alternatively, there will be attempts to marshal the best linguistic
features into one superior language; while others will suggest a return to
some form of linguistic Lowest Common Denominator.
    As I see it, Glosa represents the thrust from this last group.  To get
man talking to man, we might have to take a few steps back - to a level of
sophistication where people from all linguistic traditions can cope.

A micro-study ... I did this unix command, and got a list
of 52 Glosa words, which I reformatted a bit below:

cut -f 3 glosatest* | grep -v '[- ]' | grep '.ekt.$' | sort -u

abjekti afekta aspekti aspekto
defekti dejekti delekta detekti dialekta dialekti direkti disekti
efekti ejekti eklekti elekti erekti expekta

perfekti perspekto plekto prefekta prefekto projekti
reflekti rejekta rekti respekta respekti retrospekti
sekta sekti selekti spekta subjekti suspekta
tekti tekto vekti vekto

It's a bit of a jungle, but by disentangling it
we can reduce the 52 chaotic words to 11 nice roots:
"fekt jekt lekt nekt rekt sekt tekt vekt flekt plekt spekt".

The prefixes are familiar: "ab ad de dia dis ex in ko per
pre pro re sub super", and some few compounds like "genu".
Then maybe some missing words like "kon-vekt" appear.

Obviously there are many other groups besides "-ect".

To reduce G-8000 to manageable size, we selekt/inspekt all
these sets, then look for more patterns among the remainder.
Maybe eliminate "-fika" words with "-fect", or the reverse.

Also apparent is the random final vowel situation.
I would make them all -e for etymological and mnemonic
reasons, but there is more to it ... crypto-suffixes!

For me, the description of G6000 in the Glosa Publications list is

"6000 Greek and Latin Words and Roots which occur in the Euro-languages and
the International Scientific Terminology"
"Readership:
This dictionary gives the meanings of Greek and Latin roots used in
medicine, biology, plant and animal names etc. Suitable for educationalists,
students, linguists and for Glosa enthusiasts."

This tells me that G6000 has never been intended to be the "heart" of Glosa.
It is just a interesting exercise to demonstrate the "Hogben Principle" that
there is no need to invent an international vocabulary as an excellent one
already exists and has been waiting to be more widely used for at least the
last three centuries (i.e. the Vocabulary of Science).

In contrast the Glosa Publications list gives as the readership for CENTRAL
GLOSA
"Readership:
Ideal for beginners of Glosa, advanced students and teachers of Glosa."

This tells me that G1000 IS the "heart" of Glosa. Hogben's aim was always
for a language for all the world, and so one having the greatest possible
word economy, and a rationally constructed word list that would cover all
the essential words of intercommunication. The language should contain a
judicious selection of general terms and descriptive periphrase for specific
uses.

Ashby and Clark write "The authors consider that the Glosa presented here in
this book (CENTRAL GLOSA) is the most important Glosa....Glosa 1000. By
calling it Central Glosa it is hoped that the reader will never lose sight
of its central importance. If Glosa is to become truly Interglosa, the
International Auxiliary Language, it must remain simple. Comfortable
westerners might quickly get lost in Mega Glosa, forgetting the baser
degrees by which they ascended."
  "We hope that students who might get involved in the seeming luxury of
Mega-Glosa will quickly work up to Central Glosa, which involves clear
thinking."
  "Glosa 1000 is not only capable of great accuracy, but is also capable of
any degree of subtlety."

My understanding of the term "Mega-Glosa" is that it is all rest of the
"international" words that we might want occasionally to use for euphony or
variety. This is said to be a very large vocabulary of words, and clearly
goes beyond those words listed in the G6000 book.

I was looking at this bit by Edgar De Wahl, written
in the constantly evolving Occidental of 1925.
It is no-way pidgin, neither is it unusually
sophisticated in sentence structure:

: Li ver economie ne consiste
: in payar vili precies a reciver brac e desvaloralia,
: ma in acquisition del max bon e durabil materie o instrumente
: quel posse persister in omni occasiones.

So here I have tried to translate into Glosa,
using only *attested* vocabulary:

: U veri economi ne konsiste
: in paga vili preci a gene brakte e no-valu kosa,
: sed in aqire de maxi boni e dura materia alo ru
: qi posi tenacio in omni okasio.

The words "economy consist tenacious/persist occasion"
are all official Glosa, not compounds of my devising.
The words "u alo ru qi" are the sorest thumbs, IMO.
But even the recognizable latinate words suffer from
lack of appropriate endings so the syntax is vague.
Probably needs more particles. But I'm tired already;
every latinate word has unpredictable spelling,
so each word must be looked-up. Oddly, only "persist"
seemed to be missing, though "receive" has only
"gain" as an equivalent, qi es ne boni. Like Ila,
Glosa tethers the user to the dictionary.

To be brief, I'll respond on this only.  Simple does not mean
childish.  Charles Dickens wrote a number of books in which people
agree to meet, in the street and elsewhere, but inexplicably he
always (I think) avoided the word "rendevous".  If I'm not
mistaken, Thomas Hardy is another good example, and I expect that
many accomplished authors would fit the pattern.  Simple writing
is forceful.  They say the Bible has a small vocabulary, yet it
deals with a great variety of public and private life.  Again
there is life in the street, there are meetings, and troops
assemble, but never rendevous.

The best writing is simple and direct, but people, those of not
the highest linguistic endowment, i.e. very, very many people, are
perpetually attracted to redundant coinings which can become
fashionable.  They seem essential, because they are exactly of the
context and the time, but they are totally unnecessary.

There are also redundant pairs, such as 'ox' and 'beef', 'sheep'
and 'mutton', that seem essential to English speakers, yet result
from a non-linguistic cause, an accident of history.

If we don't carefully exclude what is really inessential, what is
redundant, we insupportably increase the vocabulary of the
language.

Of course there is much of the world of the last 1000 years, and
of modern technology, that is not encompassed by the vocabulary of
the Bible.  We need new words, but we must be discriminating, not
admit what is inessential.  Our test should be severe.

AB is simply the prefix known in all IE languages, "away from",
officially included in Glosa's list of prefixes; ABS is a variant spelling
used in cases like this, where the compound is really AB-ES (essence, ES, to be)

messy too. We could use "reanalysis = popular etymology" to cheat:
pretend it is AB-SENT = "be or feel sent away from" or something.

There either are affixes or not; examination of the
vocab proves that there are both prefixes and suffixes.
Beginners only avoid them for a while, not for long,
because they need to make certain distinctions:
credible vs. credulous, believed vs. believing,
believe vs. belief, believer vs. politician, etc.

This can be accomplished either by compounds or suffixes,
but they must be productive, i.e. usable spontaneously
rather than by consulting a dictionary. This requires a
real system, which is not clearly visible to me in Glosa.
"Glosa has no grammar" is not sounding so great anymore.
It looks to me like the authors just haven't finished it.

I don't know whether to use crypto-suffixes or head-last
compounding, and I doubt they could ever mesh well;
English does not use compounding for such things:
-able, -ive, -ed, -ing, -ion, -er, -ee, etc.
Glosa should add similar true suffixes rather than the
rather silly artificial -bo -po generics, or else state
clearly what compounds to use (pote-kredi, du-kredi, etc??).
Until then I don't expect to see people writing much in it.

QUESTIONNAIRE ANSWERS FOR JAY BOWKS

> 1. I think in Glosa.
Once I get going on a translation exercise or
start writing my notes to this list in Glosa.
I find it easier to keep my word-list open just
in case memory fails me though.

> 2. Irregularities in the vocabulary prevent me from writing in Glosa.
No, irregularities are part of all languages.

> 3. A lack of clear-cut guidelines on syntax prevent me from writing in Glosa.
No but... The mechanics of Glosa afford a sample syntax
formula that one can imitate. But a grammar
book that examines these would be helpful for
some.

> 4. I do write in Glosa and am confident that I am using standardised sentence
form.
Yes. As long as I stick to the wordlists and the
mechanics I know it's good enough for use.

> 5. I do write in Glosa, but am in a state of subjective uncertainty over my
usage.
Not uncertain. I write Glosa when I feel up to
it and I have my wordlists and sample sentences
handy for reference.

> 6. As far as I know, my Glosa usage is correct, but I could be in error on
some points.
Yes, I might make errors, that's why I depend
on the wordlists and samples.

> 7. Sometimes when attempting to use Glosa, I find that there
> are some ideas I feel unable to express in Glosa.
Yes, at times, then I try re-expressing the thought
in other words. This is something that happens to me
in several languages. It is a skill to master, as I
see it. "Circumlocution" talking about the object using
other words which are familiar to you.

> 8. A better dictionary is needed before I can feel happy in attempting to use
Glosa.
Maybe. A better dictionary would help spread Glosa's
use... Something like a "Teach Yourself: Glosa" book
with a glossary included.

> 9. An instruction manual is needed before I can feel happy
> in attempting to use Glosa.
No, one can start with what is found right now on the
net. But a manual like I mention above would definitely
help spread Glosa to new speakers all over.

> 10. A basic grammar book will improve my use of Glosa
Yes, a grammar primer with exercises would definitely
give me some practice of the language so this would help.

> 11. A basic grammar book is needed before I can feel
> happy in attempting to use Glosa.
Not really. I'm happy right now. But I'd be delighted to
purchase a manual like the one I mention above.
                   
> 12. I can understand the structure of the language through
> observation of printed Glosa texts.
Yes the Glosa mechanics samples offer an easily followed
set of patterns one can imitate.

> 13. There are aspects of the language I seek further information on:
Yes and No. On one hand the limited set of words in Glosa
serve as a means easy access to the language. The fact that
it has a limited vocabulary also hinders progress. A set
of standards in word formation would definitely improve
the language. For example any word ending in English
"-tion" could be used as "-cio" in Glosa. This would
of course change the language greatly and through the
idea of word economy out the window. But it would make
Glosa very approachable by Westerners who share this
international standardised set of Latin terms. Going
further with this development one could drop the ending
and have derived the root of the verb. If, for example...
communication = "komunikacio" ergo "komunika";
transportation = "transportacio" ergo "transporta".
But this would be a definite change of the language as
it is, perhaps too great to call it Glosa anymore, more
like Kosmo-Glosa and have Catty feeling all warm and
fuzzy inside. 

>14 I wish some folks would just develop their own auxlang
>and stop trying to change Glosa. (my addition)
Definitely! The fact that Glosa is good enough to use in
actuall exchanges of messages by students of it is reason
enough to consider it worthy of acceptance as it is.
That some folks wish to keep tweaking it goes against
the development of Glosa so far. It was a project with
a copyright by two individuals, it was developed and used
and it is good enough to use. Those that wish to can make
their own language project as they desire it and call it
something else.

Well, final vowels were not given much importance by either
Ron or Wendy, according to the other message you posted.
So, it is as we surmised, and they are very easily repaired:
the rule is that they don't matter (are in "free variation")
in most cases, therefore need not be memorized.

We need to account for the few problem cases where they conflict,
that's all. But if we (thee? not me) happen to fix the finals
in an etymologically improved fashion, that can't hurt anyone's
feelings, and it can improve Glosa greatly and cheaply. Example:

15      animala      animal
234     animali      animal, beast
5       animaliza    brutalize

OK, I see Latin "animalis" as an adjectival form,
so -i is very plausible. If the Glosa word is to function
as verb/noun/adjective, maybe -i is the only good choice.

12345   ANIMALI      animal, beast, brute, brutal, brutalize

We could have fun going through the whole vocabulary.
