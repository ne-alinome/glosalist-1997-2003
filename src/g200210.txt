*  Mi ne pa ski ke u simpli forma de un akti-verbi pa habe, intra plu
hetero lingua, plu tali morfo forma.
   Kron mi grafo uti Glosa, plura tem mi este u nece - pro klari expresi -
de uti u partikla te indika mi uti de un akti-verbi sine subjekti alo
objekti.  E so, intra u-ci previo frasa mi monstra bi de plu-ci uti: ~de~ e
~te~. 
  DE - [of, about, (to)]
  TE - [in order to]
e.g. ~de uti~ = "of using" alo "(to) use"
     ~te indika~ = "in order to indicate" alo "to indicate"
   Ex u-ci, mi pote dice ke mi uto tri forma de un 'no-finiti' akti-verbi:
kursi, de kursi, te kursi.
  1. Mi hedo kursi.  [I like to run.]; [I like running.]
  2. An habe nece de kursi, ka u tosto horo. 
        [He had need of running, because of the late hour.];
        [He needed to run, because ... ] 
      ALTERNO
     An pa nece kursi.  [He needed to run.]
  3. Te kursi ad an ergo-lo, an pa loka epi an, plu latex-sola pedi-ve.
[In order to run to work, he put on ... ];
        [To run to work, he put on ... ]

with instruction, correction of assignments, and qualifying examination -
then Sid's usage is as good as the next man's.

be a criticism of Sid, but of a language that can't decide on a set of
rules for its grammar, and which leaves its usage open to the language
sense of its practitioners.

A long time ago (when trolls were underground) we discussed the ways
different languages have different ideas about what an "adjective"
really is, and about which "parts-of-speech" are really useful;
grammar as taught in most schools is especially confusing about this.
Anyway we must identify in your example sentence that WAS is not
even a real verb, it's just an auxiliary; and TALL is a predicate
adjective, modifying the subject PLUMBER; there is no object,
it is just a subject-predicate kinda thing. Glosa has just two
distinct word-classes, the particles (including BE, HAVE, etc)
and the full words (PLUMBER, HEIGHT, etc).

Most languages do not treat attributive adjectives like English.
We've elevated them to equal status with nouns and verbs, whereas
others usually treat them more like plain nouns or verbs. We must
say "the tall one" where they would say "the tall" or "it talls".
And if a Spanish speaker hears "a man tall" or "a tall man" it is
no obstacle to understanding, but we would get all upset about it.
This is not a problem in those interlanguages that permit either
noun-adjective or adj-noun word order within the noun phrase;
both are statistically equally common among world languages,
and so it seems to me that both should be permitted in Glosa.

I want to avoid the vocabulary question for now, so will just
say "plomero" without further explanation. A definite article
is also needed, so I'll pick "le" for the nonce. For the
concept of height, I guess it has to be something like "alta".
Then we'd get approximately: "Le plomero habe alta", avoiding
the English "predicate adjective" construction. Adding the
shoulders is then straightforward ... Of course "un alta plomero"
or "un plomero alta" could mean several different things, but
that's just the way adjectives work in the real world.
