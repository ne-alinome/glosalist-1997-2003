Plu mi puta de u na lingua e u id fututri.

England-lingua in plu mu sko-do, e nu mu debe uti u lingua in plu mu eko.
Sed  un Engalnd-lingua habe u no-facili verba e u no-boni gramatika. Tali
homi ne pote boni stude u lingua, sed mu pote stude u verba ordina de u
Glosa-lingua;qi (vo) ne es u fo no-facili e es simila ad u verba ordina de
un Engalnd-lingua. Il es solo un problema de id: id es facili, sed u id
deskriptio es no-facili. Mi puta; na (plu Glosa-pe) debe sempre dece; u
Glosa verba oridna es simila ad norma (simpli) verba ordina de plu lingua.
Poli Glosa-pe nu puta de problema de u fini vokali de plu Glosa verba. Mi
puta id habe u simpli solutio: Kron u verba ne kambia u semani ko hetero
fini vokali id pote es ge-uti ko ali fini vokali.
Mi puta in futuri u lingua fu es fo ge-amo de poli homi e mu fu uti id.

I agree, that is a key point, maybe the most important.

Although the Glosa syntax is easy enough, derivation of
words is difficult and arbitrary (I mean the 2-letter
suffixes, which are a-priori rather than "natural").
International words become mangled by the "phonetic"
spelling, so are not very recognizable in writing.

Other interlanguages aim for more euro-naturalism,
but are not so easy outside euro-speaking countries.
Glosa seems to want to be more "global" than "international.
It has a nice phonology for globalism. I'm not convinced
that the vocabulary is adequate, or well-designed.

But go ahead and try using it, see if people follow.
It would be nice if one of these interlanguages were
successful in the mass market (real world). Or if
some qualified linguists reformed it one last time.
Or maybe it's a lost cause. Or I could be wrong.

This whole thing about final vowels, is a big confusion
about something that should be very minor. Final vowels
are unstressed and not so clearly pronounced or heard.
In a real language they would not be reliable carriers
of semantic (or grammatical) distinctions. It is so easy
for artificial language designers to overuse/abuse them.

Glosa rejected use of final vowels for grammar, and
I've slowly come to accept and admire that decision.
But some words are only distinguished by the final vowel.
This is confusing and should be minimized, I think
everybody agrees. They are difficult to remember, which
is an unnecessary impediment to learners. They follow
no discernible etymological or other pattern.

So, I think they should be "devalued" somehow.
Syd's idea and others have been mentioned already.

present Glosa shortly.

club.  So, of course, he loves Esperanto most, and of course he will
not be very objective to his students.  But nevertheless, perhaps some
students will see the good ideas in Glosa.  I hope, the number of
Glosa-friends will grow by this seminar a bit.  And it would be good,
if Glosa gets a bit more attention of scientists, I think.

We are told that the FV is unimportant and that we can do what we want with 
it, "verba/verbe/verbi" etc and people have in glosalist. Except of course 
when theFV is in fact really important, "vola/volu". How are beginners to 
know? They could check up all possibilities "verba/e/i/o/u" or 
$vola*flight
$vola*fly(to)
$vola*fly_to
 $volatili*evaporating
 $volt*volt_electricity
 $volta*jump_over
 $volta*to-vault
 $volta*volt
$volu*want
$volu*will
$volu*willing
$volu*willingness
$volu*wish
!
The essential parts(EP) of "fini......" are "FIN VERB VOLA VOLU" I suggest 
that we add any free vowel we wish to the ends of any word that will become 
longer than 3 letters. "fini verba volai volue"
The essential letters will be spoken carefully, clearly and completely, the 
FV can be quieter and less clear but firmly enough to support the last EL. 
This will signal the end of a word and add some variety to the sound.

mi volue kontinu u grafoe de kroma seqe u-ci e fu tentai uti u-ci hetera 
metodi de fini vokali.
